[SOCCER : Qatar stretches national borders](http://www.iht.com/articles/2004/03/10/soccer_ed3__5.php?page=1)

By Rob Hughes

This week, three Brazilian soccer players — Ailton, Dede and Leandro — flew to Doha with the intention of selling their nationhood to Qatar. The money, they say, is not the important thing; it is the desire of men in mid-career to play for somebody, anybody, at the World Cup.

“Brazil does not want me,” Ailton said, “Germany is not prepared to take me. That’s why I have decided to play for Qatar, which appreciates me. I want to fulfill my dream to play for a national team.”

Ailton is 29. He is the hottest scorer in the Bundesliga, where he has struck 20 goals in 23 games for Bremen this season. Though he insists money isn’t his motivation, he will quit Bremen for a bigger salary at Schalke next season.

Ailton and Dede and Leandro, both employed by Borussia Dortmund, were welcomed in Qatar and paraded before a press conference where they spoke of craving the “brotherhood” of World Cup action.

They looked a little sheepish and left without signing any $1 million contracts, but Qatari officials declared it was “almost certain” that Ailton will pull on the pristine white uniform of Qatar against Jordan in a 2006 World Cup qualifier March 31.

Should we be surprised, shocked, or even dare to have an opinion on this affair? Sport in the modern age is a mercenary business. A Kenyan steeplechaser, Stephen Cherono, last summer changed his name to Saif Saeed Shaheen, changing at the same time his financial status for life, and then out-running his countrymen to win a gold medal for Qatar at the athletics world championships.

Cherono — or Shaheen — is having the best of both worlds. He still lives in Kenya, still breaths the clean high altitude of the Rift Valley, and runs where it is his birthright to run. Some of his old countrymen despise him; others look at the land he owns and the four all-terrain vehicles he drives and say good luck to him.

If it isn’t about money, the stuff helps.

But the runner is an individual, exercising freedom of choice in a sport and a world that allows it. The team player is, or until recently was, the embodiment of something national.

Last year, shortly after Cherono became Shaheed, the FIFA “family” met in Doha. The governing body of world soccer passed an amendment to Article 15 of its statutes, loosening the definition of nationality and allowing “young” professionals to convert from one country to another even where they might have played at under-21 level for the other in the past.

This, claimed FIFA, allowed players who had felt exploited by being selected at youth level to fulfill themselves at a more mature stage.

The new guideline came into effect in January, and in January Frederick Kanouté, Mohamed Sissoko and Lamine Sakho, all French under-21 starlets, performed for Mali, Mali and Senegal at the African Cup of Nations.

They, at least, had blood ties, either parents or grandparents from their “new” nations. Brazilians appeared in African colors, too, and they claimed residency.

The Qatar situation is different. The desert state, with less than 500,000 people but billions of petrodollars to throw at sports, has no qualms about who or what represents it. Dollars for gold medals is the offer, and it appears that the proud Qatari representative needs not even have seen the land he will represent.

After Qatar lost a World Cup qualifying game, 3-0, to Iran a few weeks ago, its hired trainer, Philippe Troussier, told L’Equipe, the sports paper in his native France, that hiring foreign players was “probably the only means to one day qualify Qatar for a World Cup.

“Naturalizations are nothing new to Qatar, 80 percent of my squad were not born in Qatar.”

He named the Frenchmen he would like to take to Doha with him, but the Brazilians arrived first.

Back in Zurich, a FIFA spokesman told Reuters that Sepp Blatter, the president, had said the Qatar moves were “against the spirit of the game.” The spokesman said the legal implications would be urgently discussed.

Blatter is in a predicament. He has presided over the loosening of FIFA statutes concerning eligibility, yet Qatar seeks shamelessly to take the interpretation too far.

On one side is Gerhard Mayer-Vorfelder, the German FA president and FIFA executive committee member, saying, “It is ridiculous that people can acquire the nationality of a country with which they have absolutely no connection. It attacks the fundamental fabric of national teams.”

On the other side is Mohamed Bin Hammam, also a FIFA executive committee man and also a personal friend and backer to Blatter. In the two elections Blatter has fought and won for the top job in FIFA, the Qatari has, in Blatter’s words “put his private plane at my disposal” to jet him around Africa canvassing votes.

So far, Hammam’s voice has not been heard on this rapidly developing embarrassment to FIFA. But, if Blatter is true to his word and curbing the Qatar exploitation is his top priority, then he might have to call out the emergency committee, the committee for ethics and fair play, and maybe the players’ status committee.

Hammam is a prominent member of the emergency and the ethics commissions. Mayer-Vorfelder sits as deputy chairman on the players’ status committee. Thank goodness money is not the important thing to the players involved — otherwise the Qatari and the German who are at crossed purposes on this issue might have to debate it as members of in the powerful finance committee of FIFA.

The contracts are being finalized in Doha and are due to be flown to Germany for the signatures of Ailton, Dede and Leandro. If and when those three lost souls get their wish to be somebody’s national heroes, the floodgates will open. And national teams, FIFA’s bedrock.

The Warners began operations in June 2005, using their private Simpaul travel company in Trinidad to strike secret deals to sell thousands of packages of rooms and tickets to agents around the world.

FIFA turned a blind eye but were forced to act when the Trinidad media revealed last December that Warner charged huge mark-ups on Simpaul ticket-and-room packages.

FIFA?s ethics committee criticised Warner?s ticket deals, concluding he had abused his position to obtain personal benefits and failed to declare his business interests.

But in March the executive committee absolved him after he claimed he had severed connections with Simpaul.

In fact, Daryan Warner continued as managing director of Simpaul and Jack Warner?s personal assistant Patricia Modeste, who gave her address as Warner?s office in Port of Spain, remained company secretary.

Through Simpaul, the Warners secretly sold ?England Packages? to David Gambier of the Abingdon-based Sports World Group, guaranteeing 900 tickets for England?s first-round matches. Another deal was struck with Miami-based Soccer Travel to supply 1,500 tickets for Mexico?s initial games.

Then Simpaul contracted to supply an astonishing 3,000 tickets for Japan?s first three matches. Hotel rooms were booked through Florida-based Kick Sports Inc ? who ordered additional tickets to sell in Europe. These middlemen began taking advances from customers as early as November 2005. Finally, the Warners struck another profitable deal to supply £1.7m worth of hotel rooms in Germany.

Ernst & Young obtained Daryan?s emails confirming details of various deals, all copied to his father.

Warner?s demands for tickets became so outrageous that the Manchester-based Byrom company, operators of FIFA?s ticket office, tipped off Ernst & Young, who had been appointed to monitor potentially suspicious deals.

Two Ernst & Young accountants twice interviewed Warner Jnr but he scoffed at their requests for detailed information required by the FIFA-Simpaul contract and said he would refuse to hand over documents.

Again FIFA took no action.

The auditors were called in again on June 20 when Swiss travel agent Antonio Gallicchio turned up at FIFA?s Berlin hotel, complaining he had not received all the 100 euro tickets that he was paying 400 euros for.

He revealed that on June 14 Warner Jnr collected 180 tickets, ordered by his father, from FIFA?s ticket office in Berlin and immediately handed them to Kick Sports.

The huge volume of tickets demanded by Warner rang alarm bells in FIFA?s ticket office and a further Ernst & Young report submitted on July 8 to FIFA listed another 292 tickets ordered and paid for by Warner Snr, then resold at huge profits.

They concluded: ?Tickets (bought by) Jack Warner were transferred or resold into the secondary market in breach of 2006 World Cup ticketing general terms and conditions.? Yet again FIFA did not act.

Warner Snr submitted a further demand for an astonishing 1,245 tickets for second-round matches. It was not fulfilled.

Four years ago Sportsmail alleged that Warner Snr had made a $350,000 profit selling World Cup tickets in 2002.

Last night, FIFA spokesman Andreas Herren declined to reveal how many tickets Warner Snr ordered or received or explain why the audit reports had been kept secret for so long. Herren claimed the matter would be discussed at this Friday?s executive committee meeting in Zurich at the request of Jack Warner ? although there is no mention of it on the agenda published last week.
